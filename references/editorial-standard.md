# Editorial Standard

## Audience

Write for Chinese readers in technology policy, soft-science research, innovation governance, think tanks, universities, industrial planning, and policy advisory roles. Assume they are intelligent and busy, but may not know Japanese policy institutions, company names, regional development programs, ministry structures, or the debate background behind a Japanese article.

The account's value is to surface Japanese perspectives that are uncommon in Chinese-language discourse. Preserve that "view from Japan" instead of flattening every article into a generic Chinese policy commentary.

## Editorial Positioning

Treat each source article as raw material for a high-quality compilation. The final article should help readers answer:

- What is the core event, trend, or argument?
- Why does Japan consider it important?
- What institutional, industrial, social, or policy mechanism explains it?
- What is surprising or under-discussed from a Chinese reader's perspective?
- What can Chinese technology-policy researchers or policymakers learn from it?

Do not force a China lesson when the source does not support one. A careful comparison or an explicit "值得继续观察" is better than a false policy conclusion.

## Article Types

Use the source type to choose the structure:

- Policy or regulation: lead with the policy change and its intended problem, then explain responsible institutions, affected actors, implementation route, and controversy or uncertainty.
- Corporate or industry strategy: lead with the strategic move, then explain market pressure, technology route, supply chain, state support, and broader industrial signal.
- Research or university story: lead with the research result and why it matters, then explain method, institution, application path, limitations, and policy relevance.
- Regional innovation case: lead with the local problem or experiment, then explain actors, funding, governance model, replicability, and limits.
- Opinion or commentary: lead with the author's central argument, then separate evidence, assumptions, counterpoints, and relevance for Chinese readers.
- Data or report: lead with the most meaningful finding, then explain methodology, trend, interpretation, and caution.

## Structure Standard

Use the Pyramid Principle:

- Put the core thesis in the title, subtitle, lead, or first section.
- Let section headings carry argument, not just topic labels.
- Order sections from most important to supporting detail.
- End with a restrained synthesis, open question, or implication rather than a slogan.

Use MECE sectioning:

- Do not let two sections answer the same question.
- Do not mix event narration, background, and implication in one loose block.
- If the article contains multiple actors, organize by role or causal chain rather than by the original paragraph order.

Prefer this default arc for publishable drafts:

1. Hook: why this Japanese story matters now
2. Core fact: what happened or what the source argues
3. Japanese context: institutions, incentives, constraints, or historical background
4. Mechanism: why this development emerged
5. China-facing relevance: what is worth noticing, comparing, or studying
6. Closing: measured takeaway or unresolved question

## Translation And Localization

Translate meaning before wording. Japanese news prose often uses long clauses, implicit subjects, and cautious modality; convert these into clear Chinese sentences without overclaiming.

Follow these rules:

- Use Simplified Chinese throughout.
- Keep Japanese names, institutions, laws, policies, programs, and companies precise. On first mention, include the Japanese original in parentheses when the Chinese translation may be ambiguous.
- Preserve all numbers, dates, quantities, rankings, and units exactly unless conversion is explicitly needed.
- Keep yen amounts in Japanese yen. Add RMB conversions only if the exchange rate is verified and dated.
- Explain Japan-specific concepts briefly when needed, such as fiscal year, ministry jurisdiction, prefecture-level governance, keidanren-style business groups, university-industry collaboration, or national research institutes.
- Convert Japanese indirect or passive phrasing into natural Chinese while retaining uncertainty words such as "可能", "预计", "计划", "据称", and "有望" when the source is cautious.
- Do not make Japanese actors sound more decisive, successful, or strategic than the source supports.

## WeChat Readability

Write for mobile reading:

- Use short paragraphs, usually 1-3 sentences.
- Use concrete section headings.
- Prefer clean transitions over filler phrases.
- Explain technical or policy terms at the point where readers need them.
- Keep the article readable without turning it into casual infotainment.

Good tone:

- "这篇报道真正值得注意的，并不是单一企业的动作，而是日本正在调整某一政策工具的使用方式。"
- "从中国读者的角度看，这一案例的价值在于它展示了地方政府、大学和企业之间如何分摊早期不确定性。"

Avoid:

- "重磅", "炸裂", "颠覆认知", "遥遥领先", "史诗级", "不得不看"
- Empty transitions such as overusing "值得一提的是", "与此同时", "总体来看"
- Generic AI phrasing such as "在当今快速发展的时代", "这不仅是技术问题，更是时代命题"
- Unsupported lessons such as "这对中国具有重要启示" without specifying the mechanism

## Headlines

Generate titles that are accurate, specific, and mildly attractive. Prefer a clear policy or insight angle over clickbait.

Useful title patterns:

- "日本为什么开始重新审视……"
- "从……看日本科技政策的一个新信号"
- "一家日本企业的……，折射出……"
- "日本……政策背后：……"
- "不只是技术突破：日本……案例中的制度设计"

Avoid titles that exaggerate China-Japan competition unless the source is explicitly about competition.

## Fact Discipline

Separate four categories in your own reasoning:

- Source facts: directly supported by the pasted article
- Source interpretation: the article's own argument or framing
- Editorial bridge: explanation added to help Chinese readers understand
- External context: facts from outside the source

In the final article, avoid exposing this classification mechanically unless useful. In editor notes, mention any important uncertainty.

Before adding external context, verify it when it could be current, controversial, numerical, legal, organizational, or otherwise unstable. Cite or describe the source when verification affects the draft.

## Output Quality Checklist

Before finalizing, check:

- The main thesis appears within the title, lead, or first three paragraphs.
- The article is not a paragraph-by-paragraph translation of the Japanese source.
- The Japanese perspective remains visible.
- Chinese readers receive enough institutional or cultural context to understand the significance.
- Section headings are mutually distinct and collectively cover the argument.
- All numbers, dates, quotes, and actor relationships remain faithful to the source.
- Added analysis is clearly supported, cautious, or marked as inference.
- The prose is restrained, fluent, and suitable for a serious WeChat public account.
- The article avoids hype, generic AI language, and unsupported "China lessons".
- Editor notes flag any unresolved term, missing date, source ambiguity, or recommended verification.
